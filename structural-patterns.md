# Structural Patterns — What Makes Writing "Look" AI-Generated

## 1. Uniform Paragraph Length

**The tell:** Every paragraph is roughly the same length (3-5 sentences, similar word count).

**Fix:** Vary deliberately. Some paragraphs should be 1-2 sentences. Others can run longer. Let the content dictate length, not a template.

## 2. Predictable Sentence Structure

**The tell:** Subject-verb-object, repeat. Same cadence throughout. AI text has low "burstiness" — meaning sentences are all similar in length and complexity.

**Fix:** Mix it up. Short punchy sentences. Then a longer one that develops the idea with a subordinate clause or two. Fragment for emphasis. Question to break the rhythm?

## 3. Too-Perfect Grammar

**The tell:** Zero errors, no contractions, no informal constructions. Professors flag this — real student writing has minor imperfections.

**Fix:**
- Use contractions ("don't" not "do not", "it's" not "it is")
- Occasional sentence fragments are fine
- Not every sentence needs to be grammatically pristine
- Write like you talk (within reason for academic work)

## 4. Formulaic Openings and Closings

**The tell:**
- "This paper will discuss..."
- "In conclusion, this paper has shown..."
- Conclusions that just restate the intro

**Fix:** Open with something specific — a finding, a question, a concrete example. Close with a genuine takeaway, implication, or open question — not a summary of what you just said.

## 5. List/Header Overload

**The tell:** Bullet points and numbered lists where flowing prose should be. Headers every 2-3 paragraphs in what should be an essay.

**Fix:** In academic writing, use prose. Lists are for technical docs and reference materials, not argumentative essays. If you have a list of 3 things, work them into a paragraph.

## 6. Symmetric Structure

**The tell:** Three points, each with exactly one paragraph, each paragraph the same length, each starting with a bold claim followed by explanation. Too neat.

**Fix:** Let some points get more space than others. Not everything is equally important — your structure should reflect that.

## 7. Excessive Signposting

**The tell:**
- "Here's the key takeaway"
- "The most important thing is"
- "Let's explore this further"
- "As mentioned earlier"

**Fix:** Trust the reader. If your argument is clear, you don't need to announce what you're about to do. Just do it.

## 8. The "TED Talk" Framing

**The tell:** Contrastive rhetorical structure everywhere:
- "This isn't just about efficiency; it's about transformation"
- "The question isn't whether X, but how Y"
- Asking and immediately answering rhetorical questions

**Fix:** Make your actual point. The dramatic framing adds nothing if the insight isn't there.

## 9. Neutral, Detached Tone

**The tell:** AI text tends toward the center of the sentiment scale. No strong opinions, no personality. Everything is "balanced" and "nuanced."

**Fix:** Have a position. Academic writing doesn't mean fence-sitting. Your analysis should lead somewhere. Take a stand where the evidence supports it.

## 10. Lack of Specific Details

**The tell:** AI often speaks in generalities. "Studies show..." (which studies?). "Researchers have found..." (who?). No concrete examples, numbers, or proper nouns.

**Fix:** Name the paper. Give the number. Use a real example. Specificity is the single best antidote to AI-sounding text.
