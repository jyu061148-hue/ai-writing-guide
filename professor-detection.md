# How Professors Actually Spot AI Writing

Based on what educators report on Reddit and in academic forums (2024–2025). These are the human signals — not the software ones.

---

## 1. They Compare Against Your Baseline

Most professors compare your submitted essay against:
- Your in-class writing
- Discussion board posts
- Previous assignments
- Your general participation and vocabulary level

A student who writes rough discussion posts but submits polished, sophisticated essays raises immediate flags.

**Lesson:** Your AI-edited work needs to sound like *you*, not like a better version of a generic writer.

---

## 2. "Saying a Lot of Nothing"

The most common professor complaint: AI essays are **vague and formulaic**. They use a lot of words but don't actually say anything specific.

Red flag phrases professors mention:
- "The author might argue..."
- "The limitations may be that..."
- "This has significant implications for..."
- "Various factors contribute to..."

These hedging patterns are a dead giveaway because real students who understand the material make specific claims.

---

## 3. Fake Citations and Hallucinated Facts

Professors check references. AI fabricates them. This is probably the fastest way to get caught — citing a paper that doesn't exist.

**Always verify:**
- Every citation is real
- The cited paper actually says what you claim it says
- Page numbers are correct
- The journal exists

---

## 4. Sudden Style Shifts

Within a single paper:
- Sophisticated vocabulary in one section, simpler in another
- Formal academic tone that suddenly becomes conversational
- Paragraphs that feel "pasted in" from a different voice

This happens when students write some sections themselves and use AI for others. The seams show.

---

## 5. Failing the "Talk to Me About It" Test

Many professors now do oral follow-ups. If you can't:
- Explain your thesis in your own words
- Discuss why you chose a specific source
- Elaborate on an argument when pushed
- Answer questions about your methodology

...they'll assume you didn't write it. The best defense: actually understand what you're submitting.

---

## 6. Metadata and Revision History

Professors check:
- **Google Docs revision history** — was the essay pasted in all at once?
- **Document metadata** — creation time vs. edit time
- **Writing time** — a 3,000-word essay "written" in 20 minutes?

---

## 7. The "Too Good" Problem

Counterintuitively, **too-polished writing is suspicious**. Real student writing has:
- Minor grammatical imperfections
- Occasional awkward phrasing
- Uneven paragraph lengths
- The writer's actual vocabulary level (not a thesaurus explosion)

If every sentence is grammatically perfect and every transition is smooth, it reads as AI.

---

## 8. Everyone Submits the Same Essay

When 5 students in the same class submit essays with identical structure, similar phrasing, and the same three examples — professors notice. AI gives similar outputs for similar prompts.

---

## 9. Professor Traps

Some professors now use deliberate traps:
- **Hidden white text in prompts** — invisible instructions that AI reads and follows
- **Made-up facts embedded in prompts** — if the fake fact appears in your essay, you pasted the prompt into AI
- **Highly specific prompts** — questions tied to specific in-class discussions that AI wouldn't know about

---

## What This Means for Editing AI Text

1. **Match your voice** — the output should sound like your other writing
2. **Add specific details** from your actual knowledge and the actual sources
3. **Verify every citation** — this alone catches most AI use
4. **Understand what you're submitting** — be ready to discuss any part of it
5. **Don't submit raw AI output** — even "good" AI text has patterns professors recognize
6. **Be especially careful with hedging** — real understanding produces specific claims, not "might" and "may"
