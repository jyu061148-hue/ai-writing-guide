# Before & After Examples — AI vs Human Rewrites

Real patterns with concrete fixes. Use these as reference when editing.

---

## 1. The Stacked Simple Sentences

**Before (AI):**
> The study investigated the effects of climate change. It analyzed temperature data. It also examined precipitation patterns. The findings showed a clear trend.

**After:**
> The study investigated climate change effects by analyzing temperature and precipitation data, revealing a clear trend across both metrics.

**What changed:** Combined choppy sentences, removed repetitive "It..." openers, added specificity.

---

## 2. The Passive Voice Pileup

**Before (AI):**
> The analysis of the data was performed. A significant correlation was observed. The observation of this correlation led to the conclusion that further study is warranted.

**After:**
> We analyzed the data and found a significant correlation, suggesting further study is warranted.

**What changed:** Active voice, removed nominalizations ("the analysis of" → "we analyzed"), cut wordiness by 40%.

---

## 3. The Hedge Sandwich

**Before (AI):**
> It is important to acknowledge that the research has limitations. These limitations should be considered. They impact the generalizability of the findings.

**After:**
> The research has limitations that affect how broadly the findings can be applied.

**What changed:** Removed the "it is important to" hedge, collapsed three sentences into one, said the same thing in half the words.

---

## 4. The Template Introduction

**Before (AI):**
> In today's rapidly evolving technological landscape, artificial intelligence has emerged as a transformative force that is revolutionizing numerous industries. This paper will delve into the multifaceted implications of AI adoption in healthcare, examining both the groundbreaking opportunities and the significant challenges that lie ahead.

**After:**
> AI is changing healthcare — from diagnostic imaging to drug discovery to patient triage. But adoption has been uneven, and the gaps between promise and practice are worth examining.

**What changed:** Cut every AI buzzword (transformative, revolutionizing, groundbreaking, delve, multifaceted, landscape). Started with concrete examples. Used a real voice.

---

## 5. The TED Talk Framing

**Before (AI):**
> This isn't just about improving efficiency; it's about fundamentally reimagining how we approach patient care. The question isn't whether AI will play a role in healthcare, but how we can harness its potential while safeguarding ethical standards.

**After:**
> The real question is practical: where does AI actually help patients, and where does it introduce new risks? Efficiency gains matter, but so do the tradeoffs.

**What changed:** Removed the "This isn't just X; it's Y" structure (known AI pattern). Replaced dramatic framing with a direct question. Cut "harness" and "fundamentally reimagining."

---

## 6. The Exhaustive Conclusion

**Before (AI):**
> In conclusion, this paper has explored the various dimensions of AI in healthcare, examining both opportunities and challenges. As we have seen, AI offers transformative potential across diagnostic, therapeutic, and administrative domains. However, it is important to note that significant ethical considerations must be addressed. Moving forward, it is essential that stakeholders collaborate to ensure responsible implementation. Overall, the future of AI in healthcare remains promising yet requires careful navigation.

**After:**
> AI in healthcare works best where the data is clean and the task is narrow — radiology, pathology, drug interaction screening. The harder problems are organizational, not technical: who's liable when the model is wrong, and who pays to retrain it. Those questions don't have obvious answers yet.

**What changed:** Removed "in conclusion," "as we have seen," "it is important to note," "moving forward," "overall" (all AI markers). Replaced vague summary with a specific, opinionated take.

## 7. The Vague Claim

**Before (AI):**
> Studies have shown that social media has a significant impact on mental health among young people, affecting their well-being in various ways.

**After:**
> Twenge et al. (2018) found that teens who spent 5+ hours daily on social media were twice as likely to report depressive symptoms compared to those who spent one hour or less.

**What changed:** Named the study. Gave the number. Made a specific claim instead of a vague one. This is nearly impossible for AI to fake well.

---

## 8. The Monotone Paragraph

**Before (AI):**
> Machine learning models require large datasets for training. These datasets must be carefully curated to avoid bias. Data quality is essential for model performance. Poor data leads to poor predictions. Therefore, organizations should invest in data governance.

**After:**
> Machine learning is only as good as its training data. Feed a model biased data and you get biased predictions — garbage in, garbage out. That's why data governance isn't optional; it's the foundation everything else rests on.

**What changed:** Varied sentence length, added a colloquial phrase ("garbage in, garbage out"), used a semicolon, made it sound like a person wrote it.

---

## 9. The Qualification Overload

**Before (AI):**
> While it could be argued that there are potential benefits to this approach, it is equally important to consider the possible drawbacks. Some researchers suggest that the advantages may outweigh the disadvantages, though this remains an area of ongoing debate. Certainly, more research is needed to fully understand the implications.

**After:**
> The approach has clear benefits, but the costs aren't trivial. Whether it's worth it depends on context — and the current evidence isn't conclusive either way.

**What changed:** Cut hedging words (could be argued, potential, possible, equally important, certainly). Took a position instead of dancing around one.

---

## Key Takeaways

1. **Specificity beats generality** — name the study, give the number, cite the source
2. **Active voice beats passive** — "we found" not "it was found"
3. **One strong sentence beats three weak ones** — combine and cut
4. **Have an opinion** — academic ≠ neutral
5. **Read it aloud** — if it sounds like a press release, rewrite it
